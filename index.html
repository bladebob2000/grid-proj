The Ethical Core of Comedy

Is Dark Humor Morally Permissible?

Humor can help us cope — but it can also cause harm. This project explores where moral boundaries form through major ethical frameworks.

Consequences

Utilitarianism

Mill, John Stuart. Utilitarianism. 1863.

Utilitarianism evaluates morality by outcomes — whether an action increases happiness or suffering. Dark humor is morally permissible when it creates relief or connection without causing meaningful harm.

Kantian Ethics

Johnson & Cureton. Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy. 2025.

Kantian ethics centers on dignity and autonomy. Humor becomes immoral when it treats people as objects for entertainment rather than as fully human individuals with moral worth.

Ethical Subjectivism

Rachels, James. “Subjectivism in Ethics.” Purdue University.

Ethical Subjectivism argues that moral judgments reflect personal attitudes rather than objective truths. This makes the “line” for dark humor depend on the individual — and that creates real limitations.

Psychological Impact

Brigaud & Blanc. National Library of Medicine. 2021.

Research suggests dark humor can reduce emotional resistance to moral violations, making harm feel less severe. That can help coping — but it may also dull moral sensitivity.

In Defense of Dark Humor

Bills, Rebekah. Providence. 2025.

Dark humor can function as survival language in intense environments. The strongest defense argues it doesn’t erase the horror — it makes it bearable.

Final Conclusion

Dark humor is neither inherently moral nor immoral. Its permissibility depends on intent, context, and impact.

Used to connect or cope, it can be ethically justified. Used to exploit or dehumanize, it becomes morally wrong.
