Is Dark Humor Morally Permissible?

Exploring moral judgment through competing ethical frameworks

Utilitarianism Kant Subjectivism Psychology Defense

Utilitarianism — John Stuart Mill

Mill, John Stuart. Utilitarianism. 1863.

Utilitarianism judges morality by consequences, specifically the balance of happiness over suffering. From this perspective, dark humor can be morally acceptable when it creates more amusement, relief, or social connection than harm. However, when jokes produce emotional pain or reduce overall well-being, they become morally impermissible. This framework evaluates dark humor by its real-world effects rather than surface offensiveness.

Kantian Ethics — Dignity & Respect

Johnson, Robert, and Adam Cureton. Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy.

Kantian ethics emphasizes autonomy and human dignity. Actions are moral only if they treat individuals as ends in themselves rather than as objects or tools. Dark humor that dehumanizes or exploits others for laughter would violate Kant’s principles. Humor that respects autonomy and could be universalized may be morally permissible.

Ethical Subjectivism — James Rachels

Rachels, James. “Subjectivism in Ethics.” Purdue University.

Ethical Subjectivism holds that moral judgments reflect personal feelings rather than objective facts. Applied to dark humor, this suggests that a joke is morally acceptable if an individual feels comfortable with it. However, Rachels critiques this theory by showing that it cannot explain genuine moral disagreement or justify moral reasoning, revealing the limits of purely subjective evaluation.

Psychology — Dark Humor & Moral Judgment

Brigaud, Emmanuelle, and Nathalie Blanc. National Library of Medicine.

Psychological research suggests that dark humor can reduce emotional resistance to morally difficult actions by making violations feel less severe. While this can help individuals cope with stress, it also raises ethical concerns when humor dulls moral sensitivity. These findings suggest that dark humor carries moral risk when it normalizes harm.

In Defense of Dark Humor

Bills, Rebekah. Providence.

Dark humor can function as a coping mechanism in disturbing or morally complex situations. Rather than trivializing suffering, it can help individuals confront harsh realities without emotional collapse. When used to foster awareness, resilience, or solidarity, dark humor may be morally permissible and even valuable.
