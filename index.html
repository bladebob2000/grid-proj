Is Dark Humor Morally Permissible?

Explore the question through different ethical frameworks

Utilitarianism — John Stuart Mill

Mill, John Stuart. Utilitarianism. 1863.

From a utilitarian perspective, morality depends on consequences, specifically how much happiness or suffering an action produces. Dark humor can be morally acceptable if it creates more amusement, relief, or connection than harm. However, when jokes cause clear emotional pain or reduce overall well-being, they become morally wrong. This framework judges dark humor by its real-world effects, not by whether it appears offensive at first glance.

Kantian Ethics — Dignity & Respect

Johnson, Robert, and Adam Cureton. Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy.

Kantian ethics focuses on respect for autonomy and human dignity. Actions are moral only if they treat people as ends in themselves, not as objects or tools. Dark humor that dehumanizes or exploits others for laughter would be morally impermissible. However, humor that does not demean individuals and could be universalized may align with Kant’s moral principles.

Ethical Subjectivism — James Rachels

Rachels, James. “Subjectivism in Ethics.” Purdue University.

Ethical Subjectivism claims that moral judgments are expressions of personal feelings rather than objective facts. Applied to dark humor, this means a joke is morally acceptable if the individual finds it acceptable. However, Rachels criticizes this view by showing that it cannot explain genuine moral disagreement or provide reasons for moral criticism. This highlights the limits of judging dark humor solely by personal reaction.

Psychology — Dark Humor & Moral Judgment

Brigaud, Emmanuelle, and Nathalie Blanc. National Library of Medicine.

Psychological research shows that dark humor can reduce emotional resistance to morally difficult actions by making violations feel less serious. While this can help people cope with stress, it also raises ethical concerns when humor encourages harmful attitudes. This evidence suggests that dark humor is morally risky when it dulls moral sensitivity.

In Defense of Dark Humor

Bills, Rebekah. Providence.

Dark humor can serve as a coping mechanism in disturbing or morally challenging situations. Rather than trivializing suffering, it can help people confront harsh realities without emotional collapse. When used to foster awareness, resilience, or solidarity, dark humor may be morally permissible and even valuable.
